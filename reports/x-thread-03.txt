1/ Two weeks ago I got 100% test accuracy on a model that aligns cancer cell lines with human
   patients. A clean result like that is a reason to get suspicious, not a reason to celebrate. So I
   spent the next two weeks trying to break it. 🧵

2/ Check 1: is 100% just a lucky 38-cell-line test set? Reran the full pipeline on 10 independent
   random splits. Mean kNN@5 = 95.0% ± 3.4%, 95% CI [93.2%, 97.1%]. Worst seed still hit 89.5%.
   Holds up.

3/ Check 2, the uncomfortable one: does the alignment even matter? Ran real batch correction
   (Harmony, 84.2%, beaten by +15.8 pts). Then ran a plain classifier with ZERO cross-domain
   alignment, just CCLE-trained, tested directly on patients: 97.1%. My model's actual edge: 2.9
   points. Coarse cancer lineage is nearly trivial to separate. This is the number that matters most.

4/ Check 3: make it actually hard. Scaled from 3 to 15 cancer types, including pairs that are
   genuinely easy to confuse (lung LUAD/LUSC, colorectal COAD/READ, glioma GBM/LGG).
   [attach confusion_matrix_15.png]
   kNN@5 drops to 78.4% (expected). 45.8% of all errors land on those named biological pairs,
   despite them being only 3.8% of possible mistakes. 12x enrichment. When it's wrong, it's wrong
   toward real biology.

5/ Check 4: is this secretly detecting "pure cell culture vs contaminated tumour" instead of cancer
   type? Correlation with tumour purity: r = -0.455, moderate. But retrieval holds at 100% in BOTH
   high- and low-purity patient halves. Not a purity detector in disguise.

6/ Check 5, the real negative control: shuffle which cell-line cancer type maps to which patient
   cancer type, destroying the real signal. 100 shuffled runs. Best ever: 17-20%. Average: ~7%,
   right at chance. My real result (78.4%) is nowhere near that range. p = 0.0099.

7/ Check 6: tried to benchmark against Celligner, the published method for this exact problem.
   Blocked, its PyPI package has an unresolvable dependency and the source build needs R, which I
   don't have. Reporting that as a gap. If Celligner (unsupervised) matches me (supervised), that
   actually supports my point, not threatens it.

8/ Check 7: does any of this predict drug response? BRAF-mutant melanoma lines sit weakly closer to
   BRAF-mutant patients (p = 0.047). Does that proximity predict vemurafenib sensitivity? No
   (p = 0.19, n = 41). Checked embedding vs raw expression on the same task: neither recovers the
   signal at this n. Inconclusive, not a clean rescue.

9/ Where this lands: a model that reliably, robustly finds cancer lineage, mostly because that
   turned out to be a nearly-easy signal, and that does not yet reach the drug-response question
   that would make it clinically useful. Not a clinical tool. Not tested outside TCGA. Named as such.
